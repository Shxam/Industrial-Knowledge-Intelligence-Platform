INCIDENT REPORT
Equipment Failure Analysis

Report ID: IR-2026-06-15-001
Date of Incident: June 15, 2026
Location: Production Line 3 - Conveyor System
Reported By: John Martinez, Shift Supervisor
Investigation Team: Maintenance Department, Safety Department

====================================
INCIDENT SUMMARY
====================================

At 2:47 PM on June 15, 2026, the main conveyor belt on Production Line 3 experienced an unexpected shutdown due to motor overheating. The incident resulted in 4.5 hours of unplanned downtime and approximately $12,500 in lost production.

No injuries were reported. The incident was classified as a Level 2 equipment failure according to company safety protocols.

====================================
EQUIPMENT DETAILS
====================================

Equipment: Conveyor Belt Motor (Motor ID: CB3-M-001)
Manufacturer: Siemens
Model: 1LA8 315-4P
Rating: 150 HP, 1780 RPM
Installation Date: March 2019
Last Major Maintenance: December 2025

Operating Parameters:
- Normal operating temperature: 45-55°C
- Maximum allowable temperature: 80°C
- Actual temperature at failure: 95°C (estimated)
- Normal current draw: 85-95 amps
- Current at failure: 110 amps (trip setting)

====================================
SEQUENCE OF EVENTS
====================================

2:30 PM - Operator noticed unusual noise coming from conveyor drive
2:35 PM - Maintenance technician (Mike Thompson) dispatched to investigate
2:40 PM - Technician reported motor running hot (75°C by infrared thermometer)
2:42 PM - Decision made to continue monitoring while preparing for shutdown
2:45 PM - Motor temperature alarm activated in control room
2:47 PM - Motor tripped on overtemperature, conveyor stopped automatically
2:50 PM - Production supervisor notified of unplanned downtime
3:00 PM - Maintenance team began troubleshooting
6:30 PM - Motor replaced with spare, production resumed
7:15 PM - Full production rate achieved

Total Downtime: 4 hours 28 minutes

====================================
ROOT CAUSE ANALYSIS
====================================

5-Why Analysis:

Why did the motor overheat?
→ The cooling fan was not providing adequate airflow.

Why was the cooling fan not providing adequate airflow?
→ The fan blades and motor cooling fins were heavily clogged with dust and debris.

Why were the fan and cooling fins clogged?
→ The area around the motor had accumulated significant dust due to inadequate housekeeping and the ventilation system was not functioning properly.

Why was there inadequate housekeeping and ventilation?
→ The maintenance schedule did not include regular cleaning of the motor and surrounding area, and the ventilation system had not been inspected in 8 months.

Why was the maintenance schedule incomplete and ventilation system uninspected?
→ There was no assigned responsibility for motor area cleaning, and the preventive maintenance program did not include ventilation system checks for auxiliary equipment.

====================================
CONTRIBUTING FACTORS
====================================

People:
- Lack of awareness about motor cooling requirements
- No specific assignment of cleaning responsibilities
- Maintenance technicians focused on primary equipment

Process:
- Incomplete preventive maintenance schedule
- No inspection checklist for motor area cleanliness
- Ventilation system maintenance not included in PM program
- Delay in responding to initial noise report

Equipment:
- Motor located in dusty environment
- Ventilation system failed (blocked intake)
- No temperature monitoring until alarm activated
- Cooling fan design susceptible to clogging

Environment:
- High dust generation from nearby processes
- Poor ventilation in motor area
- Ambient temperature 32°C (summer conditions)
- No local exhaust ventilation

Management:
- PM schedule did not include motor cleaning
- Resource allocation prioritized production equipment
- No escalation procedure for unusual noises
- Training gaps on motor thermal management

Materials:
- Dust accumulation from process operations
- Inadequate air filtration
- No protective covers on motor intake

====================================
IMMEDIATE CORRECTIVE ACTIONS
====================================

1. Replaced failed motor with spare unit
2. Cleaned cooling fan and fins on all similar motors
3. Cleared ventilation system blockage
4. Added temporary local exhaust fan
5. Implemented daily motor temperature checks
6. Updated operator rounds checklist to include visual inspection

Status: All immediate actions completed by June 16, 2026

====================================
LONG-TERM CORRECTIVE ACTIONS
====================================

1. Update PM Schedule (Due: June 30, 2026)
   - Add weekly motor area cleaning task
   - Include monthly ventilation system inspection
   - Add quarterly infrared scanning of all motors
   Responsibility: Maintenance Supervisor

2. Engineering Improvements (Due: August 31, 2026)
   - Install motor intake filters on all critical motors
   - Upgrade local ventilation system
   - Add continuous temperature monitoring with trending
   - Install dust suppression system in conveyor area
   Responsibility: Engineering Manager

3. Training Program (Due: July 31, 2026)
   - Develop motor thermal management training module
   - Train all maintenance technicians on cooling system maintenance
   - Include in new employee orientation
   Responsibility: Training Coordinator

4. Documentation Updates (Due: July 15, 2026)
   - Update equipment maintenance procedures
   - Revise operator inspection checklists
   - Create motor cleaning procedure document
   - Update critical spare parts list
   Responsibility: Maintenance Manager

5. Monitoring and Alerting (Due: July 31, 2026)
   - Install permanent temperature sensors with 4-20mA output
   - Configure trending and alarm in DCS
   - Set early warning at 65°C (not just trip at 80°C)
   - Create mobile alerts for operators
   Responsibility: Instrumentation Supervisor

====================================
LESSONS LEARNED
====================================

1. PM schedules must include auxiliary equipment, not just primary production equipment

2. Temperature monitoring should include early warning alerts, not just trip setpoints

3. Environmental conditions (dust, temperature) must be considered in maintenance planning

4. Operator observations (unusual noises) should trigger immediate investigation, not continued monitoring

5. Cooling systems are critical to motor reliability and require regular inspection

6. Ventilation systems supporting production equipment require preventive maintenance

7. Clear responsibility assignments are needed for housekeeping in all plant areas

8. Spare parts availability prevented extended downtime - critical spares policy validated
